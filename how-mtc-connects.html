How MindTheClub Connects You

Message content in MindTheClub travels directly between two devices over an encrypted peer-to-peer channel. No MindTheClub server, and no third party, stores or relays the content of your messages.

To establish that direct connection, two devices need to exchange a small amount of setup information and, if one device is asleep, a signal to wake it. Those two supporting steps are the only places a server is involved, and below we describe precisely what each one can and cannot see.

Before any of that happens, the two devices need to know each other's identity. We describe that first, because every later step in this page builds on it.

Step 0 — How two peers verify each other

Every encrypted step that follows on this page relies on the same assumption: each peer is using the real public key of the other peer, not one substituted by a provider in between. We achieve this with one out-of-band exchange and one sealed reply, so both sides are verified after a single QR scan.

The QR code carries a fingerprint

When you generate your QR code in MindTheClub, the code contains your user identifier and a fingerprint of your public key. The fingerprint is a short cryptographic hash that uniquely identifies your key. You share this QR with someone the way you'd share any other code, by showing the screen, sending it through another channel, or any means you trust.

The scanner verifies your key

When the other person scans your QR, their device fetches your public key from the directory and computes its fingerprint locally. If that computed fingerprint matches the one in the QR, the key is accepted. If it does not match, the key is rejected and not stored. A provider substituting a different key for you would produce a mismatch and the scanner's device would refuse it.

The scanned peer is verified in return

After the scanner has verified your key, their device sends you a contact request. Inside that request, the scanner's own fingerprint is included encrypted with your verified public key. Only you can decrypt it, because only you hold the private key that matches. Your device then performs the same check in the other direction: it fetches the scanner's public key from the directory, computes its fingerprint, and compares it to the fingerprint they sealed inside the request. If they match, the contact is accepted. If they do not, the connection is refused.

What this gives us

After one QR scan, both peers have independently verified each other's public key. From this point on, every "encrypted with the recipient's public key" sentence in the steps below refers to a key that both peers know is correct. A provider that holds the directory cannot substitute either key without breaking a fingerprint check or a decryption, neither of which it has the private material to do.

Step 1 — The peer-to-peer channel (where your data goes)

MindTheClub uses WebRTC data channels to carry messages. A WebRTC data channel is a direct, encrypted connection between two devices. Once it is established between two peers:

  • Message text, images, files, and everything else flow device-to-device.
  • The transport is encrypted in transit (DTLS, the standard WebRTC encryption).
  • No MindTheClub server is in the path. There is no inbox on our side, no message database, hand over, or breach, because the content never reaches us.

Large files are split into chunks and sent over the same direct channel, with retry logic on the sending device to guarantee eventual delivery. The chunks travel peer-to-peer like everything else.

What about when peers can't connect directly?

Sometimes two devices cannot open a direct path (restrictive NATs, mobile carrier firewalls). In that case WebRTC falls back to a TURN relay to pass the encrypted media through. MindTheClub uses Cloudflare's TURN service for this fallback. The relay forwards encrypted packets, it is a standard part of how WebRTC works when a direct connection isn't possible. This fallback affects the network path of the encrypted stream only; it does not change the fact that the content is end-to-end between the two devices.

Step 2 — Signalling (how the two devices find each other)

Before a WebRTC channel can open, the two devices must exchange "signalling" information: connection descriptions and candidate network addresses (in WebRTC terms, the SDP offer/answer and ICE candidates). This is a universal requirement of WebRTC, every WebRTC application, including video-call sites, does this. The information is small and is only needed to establish the connection, it is not your message content.

The question is who carries that signalling, and what can they see. In MindTheClub:

  • Signalling is carried by a Cloudflare Worker, a small program running on Cloudflare's edge.
  • The connection between the two devices is a short-lived WebSocket to that Worker. It exists only for the few seconds needed to set up the call, then closes.
  • The Worker is ephemeral, it holds the two participants' signalling messages in memory only, for the duration of the exchange, and keeps nothing on disk. When the WebSocket closes, the rendezvous is gone.

What the signalling relay can see

We designed the signalling, so the relay learns as little as possible:

  • The signalling payload is encrypted. The SDP and ICE data (which includes device network addresses) is encrypted with the recipient's public key before it leaves the sender's device. The relay forwards ciphertext, it cannot read the connection details inside.
  • Identities are not in the clear. The two participants are addressed by opaque per-session identifiers, derived by hashing a random per-session channel ID together with the user ID. Because the channel ID is random and different for every session, these identifiers do not repeat and cannot be linked back to a user or correlated across sessions by the relay.
  • MindTheClub will only send signalling encrypted to a verified recipient key (see Step 0). If no verified key is available, the attempt is refused rather than falling back to anything weaker.

What the signalling relay can still see

Being precise: the relay receives a network connection from the sender, so it sees the sender's IP address at connection time, like any server a device connects to. It does not see message content (that never goes through it), it does not see the decrypted connection details (those are encrypted), and it does not see user identities (those are opaque per-session hashes).

Step 3 — Waking a sleeping phone (push)

If Bob's phone is asleep or the app isn't running, Alice's device needs a way to wake it so it can join the connection. For this, MindTheClub uses Firebase Cloud Messaging (FCM), Google's push service, because it is the mechanism Android provides to reliably wake an app from the background.

How the wake-up is sent

  • The wake-up is not sent from the device directly to Google. It is sent to a Cloudflare Worker, which forwards it to Google's push service. So, from Google's side, the request to send a push arrives from Cloudflare, not from the sender's device.
  • The wake-up notification is a wake-up signal only, it carries no message content. The actual message is delivered afterward over the peer-to-peer channel (Step 1).

Sealed sender

The contents of the wake-up payload are encrypted with the recipient's public key before leaving the sender. Critically, the sender's identity is inside that encrypted payload, it is not visible to the push infrastructure. This is a "sealed sender" design, the party delivering the push cannot see who sent it.

To deliver a push, any push system must know who receives it, that's how delivery works, and it is true of every push-based messenger. So, the recipient's address is necessarily visible to the push layer. What is not visible is the sender's identity, which stays sealed inside the encrypted payload.

Step 4 — Connection credentials

To establish the WebRTC connection, devices need short-lived credentials for the STUN/TURN service (the components that help two devices find a network path to each other). These credentials are generated using a secret key that must be kept off the device.

MindTheClub fetches these credentials from a Cloudflare Worker that holds the secret server-side. No Google service is involved in obtaining connection credentials. If this fetch fails, the connection falls back to public STUN servers, which only help a device discover its own public address and learn nothing about who it is contacting.

The shape of the design, in one paragraph

Putting it all together: peers verify each other's keys with one QR scan, then exchange small amounts of encrypted setup information through a stateless relay and a sealed wake-up push. Once the direct channel is open, message content flows device-to-device only. The infrastructure that helps the connection happen sees a connection arriving from an IP address, an opaque session identifier, and a payload it cannot read. It does not see who is talking to whom by identity, and it does not see what is said. That separation between "what the network sees" and "what the conversation contains" is the property MindTheClub is built to give you.
